Executive Summary

A genuinely good shop, now operating at a size that tests its own discipline.

The Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund is, by any reasonable measure, the most Buffett-Munger-coded actively managed fund in India. It runs a concentrated portfolio of roughly thirty businesses, holds material cash for opportunity, mandates international exposure for diversification, has refused style drift across thirteen years, and posts unusually high "skin in the game" — sponsor and management money invested alongside unitholders. The fund manager, Rajeev Thakkar, has compounded capital at approximately 19% CAGR since launch versus the Nifty 500 TRI's ~15% — four hundred basis points of annual excess return, compounded for thirteen years. By construction and by record, this is the kind of vehicle a Berkshire-trained analyst would find immediately recognizable. The problem, if we may call it that, is success itself. The fund crossed ₹1 trillion in assets in May 2025 — the first active scheme in India to do so — and now manages roughly ₹1.4 lakh crore. At this size, the universe of investable mid-caps shrinks meaningfully, position sizing forces large-cap concentration, and the SEBI cap on international investment (in force since 2024) has effectively frozen the fund's most distinctive lever. Recent one-year returns are essentially flat (~7%) against a near-flat benchmark, and risk-adjusted statistics have turned negative on a trailing basis. None of this destroys the thesis. But it does mean an investor entering today is buying a different fund than the one that built the reputation. Our verdict, accordingly, is a clear-eyed yes — with full acknowledgment of what has changed.

I · MECHANICS

Fund Structure & Mechanics

Before assessing the manager's judgment, the first duty is to understand the rules within which he operates — and where those rules give him room, where they constrain him.

What this fund is, plainly

The Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund is an open-ended equity scheme launched on 24 May 2013 by PPFAS Mutual Fund, an asset manager founded in 1992 by the late Parag Parikh, a respected figure in Indian value-investing circles. The current investment team is led by Rajeev Thakkar (fund manager since inception), with co-managers Raj Mehta, Raunak Onkar, Rukun Tarachandani, Aishwarya Dhar, Tejas Soman, and Mansi Kariya. Neil Parikh — Parag's son — has been CEO since 2015.

By SEBI classification, it is a flexi-cap fund, meaning at least 65% of assets must be in equity and the manager may invest across large-, mid-, and small-cap securities at his discretion. PPFAS's particular wrinkle — and it is a meaningful one — is a mandate permitting up to 35% in international equity, the most generous such allowance in the category.

This earns the fund its initial benefit of the doubt before any holdings are even examined.

III · PRICE

Valuation Discipline

A wonderful business at a foolish price is a foolish investment. The deeper question is not "are these good businesses" — they are — but "did the fund pay sensible prices, and is it doing so today?"

The fund's documented valuation framework

PPFAS has been notably explicit about its approach in its investor communications, factsheets, and the late Parag Parikh's writings. The framework has three sturdy pillars:

  • Owner's perspective: Value the business as a long-term private owner would, using cash flows, capital allocation track record, and conservative growth assumptions.
  • Margin of safety: Demand discount to estimated intrinsic value rather than paying full price. Walk away when none is available.
  • Inversion: Ask what could go wrong, what management could waste capital on, and what could destroy value, before asking what could go right.

Current valuation context

The broader Indian market is, at present, neither cheap nor expensive on aggregate. As of 21 May 2026:

What recent portfolio actions tell us

In April 2026, the fund increased domestic equity allocation from 66.75% to 68.59%, deploying capital from its cash reserves. The largest addition was ITC, where holdings rose by 1.91 crore shares, bringing the total to approximately 24 crore shares. International exposure also edged higher (from 10.59% to 11.82%) as foreign holdings appreciated, despite the SEBI cap on fresh international purchases.

This pattern is informative. The manager is incrementally turning more constructive — buying ITC at depressed levels relative to its own history, deploying cash into existing high-conviction names rather than chasing what has run, and maintaining ~15.5% liquidity for further deployment if corrections materialize.

You don't have to swing at every pitch. The fund that has the discipline to keep the bat on the shoulder when prices are foolish, and to swing hard when they are not, is the fund worth owning over decades.

— Munger frame, paraphrased

Buffett's price test — would he buy here?

An honest application of the test produces a split answer. The fund's existing holdings — HDFC Bank near book multiples of 2.5–3×, Power Grid at ~17× earnings with a sturdy 4–5% dividend yield, Coal India at ~7× earnings with double-digit free cash flow yield, ITC at ~22× following its recent re-rating — collectively trade at portfolio-level valuations Buffett would call reasonable but not cheap.

The international book is a more difficult conversation. Alphabet at ~22× trailing, Amazon at ~40×+, Meta at ~25×, Microsoft at ~33× — these are mega-cap technology businesses at premium valuations. We address this directly in the debate section below; suffice it to say here that Buffett has been a net seller of his own Apple position in this same valuation environment.

A candid observation

The fund is not, today, "loading up at giveaway prices." It is patiently maintaining a quality book at fair-to-slightly-rich valuations, with structural cash to deploy if mean reversion occurs. This is the right posture for the conditions, but it is not the posture that delivers exceptional forward returns — those come from buying at distressed prices, which is not where this market presently sits.

Skill versus luck — the consistency test

Over thirteen years, the fund has outperformed Nifty 500 TRI by approximately 440 basis points per annum. Over thirteen years, that is the difference between ₹1 lakh becoming ₹6.7 lakh (benchmark) versus ₹10.3 lakh (fund) — roughly 50% more terminal wealth. This is not a small thing.

What is genuinely impressive is the shape of that outperformance: the fund has not delivered it by being exceptionally aggressive in bull markets. The beta of ~0.69 and the structural cash buffer mean the fund typically lags in roaring bull runs. The outperformance has come almost entirely from downside protection — losing meaningfully less than the index in 2018, 2020, and the 2022 correction. Capturing roughly 90% of bull-market upside and roughly 60% of bear-market downside, compounded for thirteen years, is how this kind of record is built.

Cycle analysis — bull vs. bear behavior

  • 2016 (Demonetisation): International book buffered the domestic shock. Fund outperformed peers materially.
  • 2018 (NBFC crisis, market correction): High cash position cushioned the drawdown. Bottom-quartile decline relative to flexi-cap peers in the downward direction.
  • 2020 (COVID crash & recovery): Drawdown matched benchmark on the way down (~-20% vs. -23%); recovery was faster due to opportunistic cash deployment.
  • 2021 (Bull run, small-cap rally): Underperformed peers who were overweight small-caps. Behaved exactly as advertised — disciplined, not chase-the-rally.
  • 2022–2023 (US tech selloff, rate hikes): International book hurt initially but rebounded. Domestic value bets (PSU, ITC) outperformed.
  • 2024–25 (SEBI international cap, choppy markets): Fund navigated the cap reasonably well; relative performance stayed top-quartile.
  • 2025–26 (current correction, flat 12 months): Outperforming peers but absolute returns are uninspiring.
The performance pattern, summarized

This fund has delivered above-benchmark CAGR over 5-, 10-, and 13-year windows primarily through volatility reduction and downside protection rather than upside amplification. For an investor evaluating long-term compounding, this is the more valuable form of outperformance — geometric returns are protected by avoiding deep drawdowns, not by chasing peaks.

V · HAZARDS

Risk Assessment

Risk is rarely what shows up in the prospectus. It is the thing nobody is talking about — the unstated assumption, the structural exposure, the manager-dependency hidden behind the brand.

1. Concentration risk

The top 15 holdings account for approximately 66% of NAV. The top 10 represent about 52%. Within the top holdings, bank exposure (HDFC + ICICI + Kotak + Axis + Bajaj Holdings, the last being substantially a Bajaj Finance/Bajaj Auto holdco proxy) clusters to approximately 24%. This is a meaningful concentration. A simultaneous banking-sector shock — for instance, an NBFC crisis spreading to private banks, or a sudden NPA cycle — would hurt this fund disproportionately.

2. Manager and key-person risk

Rajeev Thakkar has managed the fund since launch. The team has been remarkably stable — Raj Mehta has been a co-manager since 2016, and the broader investment team has not seen meaningful churn. But thirteen years is a long time. Thakkar is now a senior figure with material personal wealth tied to PPFAS. The succession question — what happens when the lead manager eventually steps back — is not addressed in any formal way that we are aware of. The CEO Neil Parikh holds the institution together, but the investment culture is, to be candid, substantially Thakkar's.

Key-person reality

The investment process is collaborative and the firm has institutionalized its approach to a meaningful degree. But if Rajeev Thakkar were to leave PPFAS tomorrow, an investor would have to seriously reconsider the position. This is true of almost every successful active fund in India — it does not disqualify the investment, but it must be acknowledged.

3. AUM scale risk

This is the most significant emerging risk and deserves its own treatment. AUM grew from ₹22,324 Cr in June 2022 to ₹1.41 lakh Cr in April 2026 — a 6.3× increase in less than four years. At this scale, several constraints bind:

  • Mid-cap and small-cap participation becomes mechanically harder. Buying a 1% position in a ₹10,000 Cr market-cap company requires acquiring ~₹1,400 Cr of stock — a 14% stake. This forces large-cap concentration regardless of where opportunity lies.
  • Liquidity in lower-quality names is now thin enough that the fund cannot establish or exit positions without moving the price meaningfully.
  • The manager's traditional small-cap "long tail" of high-conviction-but-small bets becomes impractical.

In effect, scale has narrowed the fund's investable universe and made it more large-cap-oriented than its mandate technically requires.

4. Regulatory and mandate risk

SEBI introduced a cap on Indian mutual fund overseas investment in early 2022, which has been in force in various forms since. The fund cannot freely add to its international holdings — only existing positions can be held. This neutralizes what was one of its most differentiated structural features. International exposure has appreciated to 11.82% from passive price gains, but the manager cannot meaningfully rebalance into the international book.

5. Liquidity risk for unitholders

At ₹1.41 lakh Cr, this is one of India's largest equity mutual funds. Mass redemption events have historically forced selling at adverse prices in less liquid funds. The PPFAS approach mitigates this through (a) ~15.5% liquidity buffer that can absorb meaningful redemptions without selling stocks, and (b) the exit load structure (2%/1% in years 1–2) which discourages flighty money. Unitholders themselves tend to be retail SIP investors with long average tenures, which further dampens redemption volatility.

6. Inflation and currency hedging

The fund offers reasonable inflation protection through (a) equity ownership in pricing-power businesses, (b) REITs with rent-pass-through, and (c) the international USD-denominated book which protects against INR depreciation. The latter is structurally now capped, but the existing book retains some hedging value.

7. Tail risk — what breaks this fund?

Inverting the question, as Munger would, what is the actual scenario in which this fund delivers serious capital loss? Several real possibilities:

  • Indian banking system stress (similar to NPA cycle of 2015–17): Banking + financials concentration of ~24% would compress meaningfully. Recovery historically takes 18–24 months.
  • PSU re-rating reversal: Power Grid + Coal India + ITC together = ~18%. A policy shock (e.g., tax changes on cigarettes, energy transition acceleration, dividend payout cap) could compress these together.
  • US mega-cap technology drawdown: A 2022-style tech selloff would meaningfully hurt the 11.82% international book; international cap means the fund cannot rebalance opportunistically as it could in 2022.
  • Mass redemption event: Lower probability given investor base and exit loads, but at this AUM the absolute rupee value of redemptions in a panic could force selling.
  • 2008-style global financial crisis: Cash buffer would provide some cushion, but a -40% drawdown is not implausible in a true global crisis.
VI · DIALOGUE

Three Substantive Debates

Here are three places where Buffett and Munger would honestly differ on this fund. Each disagreement illustrates a distinct dimension on which a thoughtful investor must form their own judgment.

Debate I The Question of Scale
Buffett — The Skeptic on Size
"₹1.4 lakh crore is a problem masquerading as a milestone."

I have written many times that size is the enemy of returns. Berkshire today cannot replicate what Berkshire did in 1965, and I have admitted that openly. PPFAS is now facing the same arithmetic. When a fund grows from ₹22,000 Cr to ₹140,000 Cr in four years, the universe shrinks. The manager can no longer take meaningful positions in mid-cap or small-cap businesses without distorting their prices. The fund is becoming, by default, a large-cap fund with international flavoring.

The compounding math is unforgiving. If the manager could deliver 5% excess return at ₹22,000 Cr, perhaps he can deliver 2–3% excess return at ₹140,000 Cr, and approach 0% at ₹300,000 Cr. An investor entering today is buying a different vehicle than the one that built the reputation.

Munger — Defender of the Operator
"Size is real, but discipline can be larger than size."

You are correct in principle, and there is a real constraint here. But you are oversimplifying. Thakkar's edge has never been small-cap obscurity. His edge has been refusing to chase, sitting in cash when nothing is cheap, owning ten to fifteen high-quality businesses he understands deeply, and not panicking when others do. Those qualities scale better than stock-picking acumen does.

Look at the actual portfolio. The top holdings are large-cap quality compounders that have plenty of liquidity at this size. The manager is not forced into bad ideas by scale — he is simply forced to concentrate on the best ones. That is not necessarily a degradation. It may, in fact, be a clarification. The fund will produce lower future excess returns than it did historically. That is mathematically certain. But the absolute return will still be respectable, and the downside protection that defines the franchise does not depend on stock-level alpha. Discipline scales.

Synthesis

Both correct in part. The fund's future excess return is structurally capped relative to its history — accept this. But the fund's downside protection, quality bias, and behavioral discipline do scale. Investor expectation calibration is the key: 4% historical alpha will likely become 2% prospective alpha, while the structural advantages persist.

Debate II The PSU Value Bet
Buffett — The Cash-Flow Realist
"Coal India and Power Grid generate enormous cash. That is the business."

I have owned regulated utilities. I own railroads. I own companies that operate within government frameworks. The question is not whether a business is government-influenced — it is whether the cash flows are durable and the price is reasonable. Power Grid is essentially a monopoly on inter-state electricity transmission with regulated returns and a 4–5% dividend yield. Coal India is the dominant fuel supplier to a country that will burn coal for at least another fifteen to twenty years, regardless of energy transition rhetoric, and it returns capital aggressively.

At seven to eight times earnings with double-digit free cash flow yields, these are not glamorous. They are not high-growth. But they are durable cash machines bought at sensible prices. That is precisely the kind of investment that allows a portfolio to compound steadily through cycles without depending on multiple expansion.

Munger — The Inversion Critic
"Invert. Ask what destroys these businesses, not what sustains them."

I am less sanguine, and I will tell you why. Coal India is the largest single holding at 5.95% of the fund. Power Grid is 6.99%. Together with ITC's regulatory exposure, these PSU and quasi-PSU bets represent roughly 18% of the fund.

Now consider the inversion. What destroys Coal India? Energy transition arriving faster than consensus, carbon taxes, a policy shock on dividend payouts, ESG-driven institutional outflows, or simply a government decision to use the company as a fiscal instrument by raising royalties or capping prices. What destroys Power Grid? Regulatory return cuts when the political winds shift. What destroys ITC? A genuine policy attack on cigarettes, which has been threatened in India repeatedly. These are not low-probability tail risks. They are non-zero, partially correlated risks bundled into 18% of the fund. Buying these businesses for current cash flow is rational. Sizing them so large that a simultaneous policy shock dents a fifth of the portfolio is less obviously rational.

Synthesis

The PSU bets are individually defensible at current prices. The aggregate sizing is the real question, and reasonable people can disagree. The fund manager has clearly weighed this and chosen current cash yield over diversification — a conscious decision that becomes a problem only if a correlated policy shock arrives, which is not the base case but is not zero probability either.

Debate III The Frozen International Book
Buffett — On What Cannot Be Helped
"The regulatory cap is what it is. Hold the position; don't apologize for it."

The fund's 11.82% holding in Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft is, by any reasonable measure, four of the highest-quality businesses in the world. The fund acquired these positions before the SEBI cap, when valuations were materially more attractive. Yes, they are now expensive — Microsoft and Amazon especially are trading at multiples that require ongoing growth to justify. But these are the kinds of businesses I would buy in size if I could.

The fund cannot add to these positions today. It also has not been forced to sell them at unattractive prices. That is a reasonable outcome. The international book continues to provide currency diversification and exposure to global growth that Indian businesses do not directly offer. Hold it.

Munger — The Diagnostic Skeptic
"You bought these at attractive prices. The valuation today is a different question."

I will press you on this. Alphabet at 22× earnings, perhaps. But Microsoft at 33×? Amazon at 40×+? The fund holds these positions at current prices because it has no choice — selling crystallizes a tax liability and forfeits the long-term thesis, while buying is regulatorily prohibited. So the position size is essentially set by what the market does, not by the manager's continuing conviction.

That is an uncomfortable place for a value-investing house to be. The fund's reputation rests partly on the international book's historical contribution to returns. Going forward, the international book will track US tech rather than express the manager's judgment. If US large-cap tech corrects 30%, the fund takes the full hit. If it goes flat, the fund gets the opportunity cost. If it goes up, the fund gets the return — but the manager wants no credit for that, because he did not choose it. The international book is now a passive bet, dressed in active clothing. The investor should adjust their model accordingly.

Synthesis

The international book is a legacy quality position that the manager cannot meaningfully rebalance. Treat it as a passive index sleeve embedded in an actively managed fund. Its forward contribution to returns will track US mega-cap tech, including the present-day valuation risk. The fund's "differentiated international exposure" advantage has been substantially neutralized by regulation, and this fact should be priced into the investor's expectations.

VII · DOCTRINE

Strategy & Philosophy

A strategy is what the fund actually does, not what its marketing claims. We assess the doctrine in operation, not the doctrine in brochures.

The stated philosophy

PPFAS articulates its approach in unusually plain language for an Indian asset manager. The investment letters, factsheets, and the annual unitholder meeting (held publicly each year by Neil Parikh) consistently emphasize: (a) businesses-first analysis rather than top-down sector calls, (b) margin of safety and avoidance of momentum chasing, (c) willingness to hold cash when valuations are unattractive, (d) international diversification where possible, (e) long-term ownership of a concentrated set of high-quality businesses, and (f) absolute return focus rather than relative-to-benchmark tracking.

The strategy in practice

An honest examination of thirteen years of portfolio statements suggests that the stated philosophy and the actual practice align unusually well — a non-trivial achievement in an industry where the gap between marketing and execution is often wide.

  • Low turnover: The portfolio composition is recognizable across multiple years. Many holdings (HDFC Bank, ITC, ICICI Bank, Power Grid, the US tech names) have been long-tenured positions, with sizing adjustments at the margins.
  • Sector discipline: The fund has not chased railways, defense, manufacturing, or any of the sector themes that drove recent Indian market returns. It missed those rallies and refused to participate.
  • Cash discipline: Cash positions have ranged from roughly 5% to 20% across cycles, deployed counter-cyclically (lower in 2020 after COVID, higher in 2021 at peak valuations, deployed again in 2022 and 2023 corrections).
  • Concentration discipline: The top 10 holdings have consistently been ~50% of the portfolio. No drift toward over-diversification despite AUM growth.

Systematic versus discretionary — and which is better here

The fund is unambiguously discretionary, not systematic. There is no quantitative screen, no factor model, no algorithm. The investment team — five to seven people — discusses ideas, debates them, and decides. The lead manager has final authority but operates within a collaborative culture.

Whether discretion is better than system depends on the practitioner. For most active managers, systematic approaches deliver better risk-adjusted returns because human discretion introduces behavioral biases and style drift. The PPFAS team is a clear exception — thirteen years of disciplined execution suggests a culture that genuinely defends against these biases. The discretionary approach also allows the kind of contextual judgment (e.g., holding cash when nothing is cheap) that no systematic approach naturally encodes.

A note on cycle adaptability

The fund has navigated four major Indian market cycles since 2013 — the 2016 demonetization shock, the 2018 NBFC stress, the 2020 pandemic crash and recovery, and the 2022 rate-hike correction. In each, its drawdown was less severe than the benchmark and its recovery was faster or equal. This is the strongest available evidence that the philosophy is real, not marketing.

VIII · COMPOUNDING

Long-Term Wealth Creation

Will this fund produce attractive real returns over the next decade? The honest answer requires distinguishing the historical record from the forward expectation.

The arithmetic of expected returns

Forward 10-year expected returns must be calibrated against (a) starting valuation, (b) earnings growth potential, and (c) the structural alpha the fund can be expected to deliver. Working through each:

Expected Nifty 500 TRI Return ~11–13% Earnings growth 12–14% + dividend yield 1.5% − modest multiple compression from current 20× P/E
Expected Fund Alpha ~1.5–2.5% Reduced from historical 4.4% due to AUM scale and frozen international book
Expected Fund 10-yr CAGR ~13–15% Subject to wide variance. Range 10–17% across plausible scenarios.

Real (inflation-adjusted) returns

India's inflation target is 4% with a tolerance band of 2–6%. Recent inflation has run 4–5%. Assuming a 4.5% long-run inflation expectation, the expected real return for this fund over the next decade is approximately 8.5% to 10.5% real CAGR. That is a respectable but not extraordinary expected real return. For an Indian investor, it compares favorably against fixed income (10-year G-Sec at ~6.8%, real return ~2.3%), but it does not promise spectacular wealth creation.

Durability across cycles

The fund's structural features — cash flexibility, quality bias, low beta, downside protection — should remain operative across future cycles. The investment culture appears strong, manager continuity is intact, and the firm has shown discipline in not launching faddish products to chase AUM growth. PPFAS has resisted launching thematic funds, sectoral funds, smallcase products, or other AUM-grabbing vehicles. This restraint is itself an investment-quality signal.

What would make this an exceptional investment?

Three conditions would meaningfully elevate the fund's forward attractiveness:

  1. A meaningful Indian market correction (-25% or more): The fund would deploy its ~15.5% cash buffer aggressively, and the entry valuations for new SIP money would improve materially.
  2. Restoration of international flexibility: If SEBI relaxed the overseas cap, the fund could once again express its global value views actively, restoring one of its key historical edges.
  3. AUM normalization: Through either redemptions or asset deflation, if AUM stabilized at a lower level, the manager's stock-picking universe would re-expand.

None of these is in the investor's control. Calibrate expectations to the base case.

The unified recommendation

The Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund earns an Investment-Grade Hold recommendation for long-term Indian equity allocations. It is, in our judgment, among the highest-quality actively managed equity vehicles in India today, with a culture, philosophy, and execution record that align unusually well with the principles a Berkshire-trained analyst would seek. The manager is disciplined, the portfolio is constructed with genuine quality bias, costs are reasonable, and the structural risk controls are real.

The reason this is "Hold" rather than "Strong Buy" is not a criticism of the fund but a recognition of its current circumstances. AUM has grown to a size that mechanically caps forward excess return. The SEBI international cap has neutralized one of the fund's distinctive levers. The underlying market is fairly valued, not cheap, so the manager is not currently operating in his most rewarding environment. None of these are flaws in the fund — they are realities of the moment.

Suitable action: Allocate 15–25% of an Indian equity portfolio to this fund as a core holding, deployed via SIP rather than lump sum, with a minimum 10-year horizon. Pair with a low-cost Nifty 500 or Nifty 50 index fund (for the broad market exposure this fund will not capture) and possibly a small-cap or mid-cap fund (for the higher-risk segments PPFAS can no longer meaningfully access).

Unsuitable action: Treating this as a guaranteed high-return vehicle, or as the sole equity holding, or as a tactical position. It is a core long-term compounder, not a tactical trade.

The key decision factors

  1. Trust in the manager. This is the single most important factor. If you trust Rajeev Thakkar and the PPFAS team to remain disciplined for another decade, this investment is sensible. If that trust is uncertain, the case weakens substantially.
  2. Time horizon. Anything under 7 years and this fund's structural cash drag will hurt you in normal markets. 10 years and longer is where the math works.
  3. Expectation calibration. The next decade will not look like the last. Plan for 12–14% nominal, not 19%.
  4. Portfolio context. This fund is a complement, not a complete portfolio. It should sit alongside index funds and possibly a small-cap allocation.

Conditions for reconsideration

Downgrade triggers

1. Departure of Rajeev Thakkar from active management without a clearly demonstrated successor
2. Style drift — meaningful sector concentration in newly fashionable themes (defense, semiconductors, etc.) inconsistent with stated philosophy
3. AUM growth to ₹2.5–3 lakh crore without corresponding manager commentary on capacity constraints
4. Sustained underperformance vs. Nifty 500 TRI for 3+ years (note: weak absolute returns alone do not qualify; relative underperformance does)
5. Material increase in expense ratio inconsistent with industry pricing

Upgrade triggers (toward Strong Buy)

1. Indian market drawdown of 25%+ allowing aggressive cash deployment
2. SEBI relaxation of overseas investment cap, restoring international flexibility
3. AUM normalization to ₹80,000 Cr or lower
4. Material widening of the discount-to-NAV opportunity in REITs and quality mid-caps

If we are being honest with ourselves, the most striking feature of this fund is not its returns. It is the rare alignment between what the fund manager says he will do, what he actually does, and what unitholders ultimately experience. In a market increasingly populated by tracking-error-minimizing closet indexers, momentum-chasing thematic funds, and AUM-maximizing distribution machines, PPFAS has remained a fund where the people running it actually think about businesses, actually demand margin of safety, and actually eat their own cooking. That is not a guarantee of future returns. But it is a guarantee of something rarer and arguably more valuable — a guarantee of intellectual honesty.

You will not get rich quickly here. You will, with reasonable probability and over a sufficiently long period, get to wealth honestly. That is exactly the form of compounding worth owning.
